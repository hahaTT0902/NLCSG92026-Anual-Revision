# Biology Topic Note — Digestion and Absorption

## Why digestion is needed
Food often contains large insoluble molecules. These are too large to pass through the wall of the small intestine into the blood. Digestion breaks them down into small soluble molecules that can be absorbed and used by the body.

## Mechanical and chemical digestion
Mechanical digestion physically breaks food into smaller pieces, increasing the surface area for enzymes. Chemical digestion uses enzymes to break chemical bonds in food molecules.

## Key enzymes
- **Amylase** breaks starch into sugars.
- **Protease** breaks proteins into amino acids.
- **Lipase** breaks lipids into fatty acids and glycerol.

## The digestive system
- **Mouth**: chewing begins mechanical digestion; saliva contains amylase.
- **Oesophagus**: carries food to the stomach.
- **Stomach**: churns food and begins protein digestion; acid helps provide the right pH and kills pathogens.
- **Small intestine**: completes digestion and absorbs nutrients.
- **Large intestine**: absorbs water.
- **Rectum / anus**: stores and removes faeces.

## Villi and absorption
The small intestine is adapted for absorption. It has many villi, which increase surface area. Each villus has a thin wall, good blood supply, and in many descriptions a lacteal for fat absorption. These adaptations make diffusion and transport faster and more efficient.

## Liver and bile
The liver makes bile, which is stored in the gall bladder and released into the small intestine. Bile neutralises acid from the stomach and creates conditions that help lipase work effectively. Bile also emulsifies fats, increasing surface area for digestion.

## Common exam ideas
- Explain why the small intestine is adapted for absorption.
- Describe the role of bile.
- Compare mechanical and chemical digestion.
- Identify which enzyme digests which food group.
