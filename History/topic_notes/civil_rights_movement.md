# History Topic Note — The Civil Rights Movement

## Background
African Americans faced segregation and discrimination in the USA, especially in the South. Legal and social barriers limited equality in education, transport, voting and public life.

## Methods used
The movement used court cases, boycotts, sit-ins, freedom rides, marches and speeches. Non-violent direct action became especially important in the 1950s and 1960s.

## Important events
- Brown v. Board of Education (1954)
- Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–56)
- Little Rock (1957)
- Sit-ins (1960)
- Freedom Rides (1961)
- Birmingham campaign (1963)
- March on Washington (1963)
- Civil Rights Act (1964)
- Voting Rights Act (1965)

## Why the movement succeeded to some extent
Success came from direct action, strong organisation, media attention and federal government intervention. Leaders such as Martin Luther King Jr. became powerful national voices, but grassroots activists were also essential.

## Limitations
Legal change did not remove all inequality. Poverty, racism and discrimination continued, which is why many historians say the movement achieved important but incomplete success.

## Exam focus
- Which was more significant: direct action or legal change?
- How successful was the movement by the mid-1960s?
- Why did federal action matter?
