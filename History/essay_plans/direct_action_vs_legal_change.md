# History Essay Plan — Direct Action vs Legal Change

## Question
Which was more significant in the civil rights movement: direct action or legal change?

## Thesis
Both were important, but direct action was more significant because it mobilised public opinion, exposed injustice, and created the pressure that made legal change possible.

## Paragraph 1 — Direct action created national attention
- Point: Direct action turned civil rights into a national moral issue.
- Evidence: Montgomery Bus Boycott, Birmingham campaign, Freedom Rides.
- Explanation: Media coverage exposed violent resistance and increased sympathy for the movement.
- Link: This made direct action a major driving force.

## Paragraph 2 — Legal change secured permanent gains
- Point: Legal change was essential in turning protest into enforceable rights.
- Evidence: Brown v. Board, Civil Rights Act 1964, Voting Rights Act 1965.
- Explanation: These changed the law nationally and gave protection beyond local activism.
- Link: Legal reform was crucial, but often came after sustained protest.

## Paragraph 3 — Why direct action was still more significant overall
- Point: Without direct action, legal change would likely have been slower and weaker.
- Evidence: Selma marches and support for voting reform; March on Washington and pressure for civil rights legislation.
- Explanation: Protest created urgency and political cost for inaction.
- Link: Therefore, direct action had the deeper causal significance.

## Counter-argument
- Legal change mattered because laws outlast protest.
- However, protest was the mechanism that made those laws politically possible.

## Conclusion
Direct action was more significant overall because it drove momentum, built pressure and changed public awareness, while legal change consolidated the gains.
